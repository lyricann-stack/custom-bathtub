Bathtub dimensions guide: standard sizes & charts

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · Kreiner Atelier

Quick answer: most built-in bathtubs are 1500–1700 mm long × 700–760 mm wide × 380–450 mm deep. Freestanding tubs commonly run 1400–1830 mm long × 700–860 mm wide with external heights of 550–610 mm. Japanese soakers trade length for depth: 1100–1400 mm long but up to 780 mm tall. Everything else is detail — and the detail is what decides whether a bath actually feels right.

External dimensions. Interior bathing space is typically 100–200 mm shorter than external length depending on wall thickness and backrest slope. Australia broadly follows the UK/EU convention, where 1700 × 700 mm is the classic built-in size; the US standard is 60 × 30 inches (1524 × 762 mm).

Length, width, depth — what each one actually does

Length sets your bathing posture. Below ~1500 mm external, most adults sit upright; from ~1600 mm a moderate recline works; at 1700 mm+ a tall bather can stretch. But interior length is what matters: a 1700 mm tub with a steep backrest can offer less legroom than a 1600 mm tub with a vertical end wall.

Width is shoulder room and arm position. 700 mm-wide tubs are snug; 750–810 mm suits most adults; beyond 900 mm you're in wallowing (or two-person) territory — glorious, but check your floor plan and your hot-water supply.

Depth decides how much of you is under water. What counts is water depth to the overflow, not external height: a 600 mm-tall tub might hold only 380 mm of water. For a proper soak, look for 400 mm+ to the overflow; Japanese soakers reach 550 mm+ by having you sit upright.

Small bathrooms: go shorter and deeper

The classic mistake in a small bathroom is squeezing in the longest tub that physically fits, then finding it shallow and joyless. The better trade is a shorter, deeper tub: a 1400 × 750 mm deep soaker gives a more immersive bath than a cramped 1600 mm shallow one, and leaves room to move. This is where custom sizing earns its keep — the tub is drawn around the room instead of the room being rearranged around a catalogue size.

Measuring your space

Measure the full wall-to-wall dimensions of the intended zone, note the waste position and floor joist direction, and allow 100–150 mm of cleaning clearance around a freestanding tub (300 mm+ on the access side is more comfortable). Check door and stair access for delivery — an 800 mm-wide tub does not enjoy a 720 mm doorway. When custom sizing makes sense

If your space is between standard sizes, your bather is taller (or shorter) than the average the catalogue assumes, or the room deserves a shape that doesn't exist off the shelf — custom is the honest answer. Frequently asked questions

What is the most common bathtub size?

The most common built-in (alcove) size is about 1500–1700 mm long × 700–760 mm wide. For freestanding tubs, roughly 1700 × 760 mm is the most popular size — it fits most adults comfortably and suits a standard family bathroom.

What is the minimum bathtub size for a comfortable soak?

Around 1200–1400 mm long works if the tub is deep (a Japanese-style soaker, 550 mm+ water depth) so you sit upright rather than recline. For a reclining soak, most adults want at least 1500 mm of external length.

What size is a two-person bathtub?

Typically 1800–2000 mm long and 900–1200 mm wide. Side-by-side bathing needs width; face-to-face bathing needs length and a symmetrical shape with centre-positioned waste and taps.

How much space should I leave around a freestanding bathtub?

Leave at least 100–150 mm between the tub and walls for cleaning, and ideally 300 mm or more on the access side. A tub pushed hard against a wall loses the sculptural effect and is difficult to clean behind.
