Bathtub ergonomics: comfort is a set of numbers

Updated July 2026 · ~8 min read · Kreiner Atelier

Two tubs with identical outside dimensions can feel completely different — one cradles you, the other feels like sitting in a bucket. The difference is a handful of interior numbers that rarely make the brochure. As a maker that shapes each tub individually, we work with these numbers every day; here they are.

1 · Backrest angle

The backrest is the single biggest comfort factor. Around 100–115° from the tub floor suits most bathing: the lower end supports your head and neck for reading or conversation; the upper end gives a fuller recline for a long soak. Ergonomic designs sometimes go to 118–121° with lumbar shaping. Beyond ~125° bathers tend to slide; steeper than ~95° feels rigid. Taller, longer-legged bathers usually prefer a slightly steeper angle than the same tub gives a shorter bather — one more argument for shaping the tub around the person.

2 · Soaking depth (measure to the overflow)

Comfort is water over your shoulders, and the overflow drain sets the legal maximum water line — not the external height. For a reclining soak, look for 400 mm+ of water depth; premium soakers reach 450–500 mm. Upright Japanese soaking is a different geometry: 550–650 mm of depth with a short footprint, often with an integrated seat. If a spec sheet doesn't state depth-to-overflow, ask.

3 · Interior length: the legroom formula

A workable rule of thumb for a reclined soak: interior length ≈ bather height − 550 to 650 mm (the knees bend slightly and the torso reclines). A 170 cm bather is comfortable from ~1100 mm interior; a 190 cm bather wants ~1300 mm+. Backrest slope eats interior length, which is why two 1700 mm tubs can differ by 150 mm of real legroom. For two-person face-to-face tubs, both ends need bathing-grade slopes and the waste moves to the centre.

4 · Width, shoulders and arms

Interior width of 560–660 mm at shoulder height suits most adults — enough to rest arms at your sides without pressing the walls. Wider is not automatically better: past ~750 mm interior you lose the gentle side support that makes a tub feel secure, and heat disperses faster in the larger water volume.

5 · Lumbar, headrest and the small radii

The details you feel but never measure: a slight lumbar bulge in the backrest keeps the lower spine's natural curve; a rolled or padded headrest zone stops the tub edge biting your neck; and a generous radius where the base meets the walls is friendlier to hips and heels than a sharp junction. In our design studio, edge roundness and body curve are sliders — these are exactly the surfaces they control.

6 · The rim: slim looks, wide lives

A slim 15–30 mm rim reads beautifully minimal; a 60–90 mm rim holds a book, a glass and your forearms. Neither is wrong — but decide consciously, because it changes daily use more than most buyers expect. (Our rim-width slider runs 15–100 mm for exactly this reason.)

Design around the bather, not the average

Catalogue tubs are shaped for a statistical average nobody actually is. If the primary bather is 155 cm or 195 cm, shares the bath, or has a bad back, the right geometry is different — and with made-to-order manufacturing it costs nothing extra to get it right. Frequently asked questions

What is the most comfortable backrest angle for a bathtub?

Roughly 100–115° from the tub floor for soaking; closer to 100–105° if you like to read or sit up, closer to 110–120° for a full recline. Steeper than ~95° feels like a chair; flatter than ~125° and you start sliding.

How deep should a soaking tub be?

Aim for at least 400 mm of water depth to the overflow for shoulder coverage while reclining. Japanese-style upright soakers work best at 550 mm or more.

How long should a bathtub be for a tall person?

As a rule of thumb, interior length should be about your height minus 550–650 mm for a comfortable recline. A 190 cm bather is happiest with roughly 1300 mm+ of interior length — usually a 1750–1900 mm external tub, or a custom interior layout.

Does rim width matter?

Yes — a 60–90 mm rim gives you somewhere to rest arms, a glass or a book, while a slim 15–30 mm edge looks minimal but offers less support. It's an aesthetic and ergonomic trade-off.
