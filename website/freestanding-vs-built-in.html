Freestanding vs built-in: which is right for you?

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · Kreiner Atelier

A freestanding tub stands finished on all sides — a piece of furniture. A built-in (alcove or drop-in) sits against walls or inside a deck, with only the interior and rim visible. Both can be excellent; they solve different problems. Here is how they actually compare in use.

1 · Space and placement

Built-ins win on efficiency: an alcove tub uses three existing walls, and its rim doubles as a shelf. Freestanding tubs need clearance — 100–150 mm minimum around the shell, more on the access side — but reward it by making the room feel deliberate and larger. Rule of thumb: if the tub zone is under ~1900 × 1100 mm, a built-in or a compact deep soaker will serve you better than a squeezed statement piece.

2 · Plumbing and taps

Alcove installs use standard wall-fed plumbing — the cheapest, simplest route. Freestanding tubs need either a floor-mounted filler (elegant, pricier, must be positioned before the slab or floor is closed) or wall taps reaching over the rim. Waste position matters too: it must line up with existing floor penetrations or the floor gets opened. None of this is difficult; all of it is cheaper decided early.

3 · Cleaning and living with it

Freestanding means more visible floor to mop and a back edge to reach if placed near a wall. Built-ins hide their sides but grow silicone joints where rim meets tile — the part that eventually discolours and needs redoing. Call it a draw, with different chores.

4 · Cost

Comparable quality costs comparable money at the tub level. The difference is installation: alcove is cheapest; drop-in adds the deck build; freestanding adds floor plumbing and, for heavy materials, possibly a floor-loading check. A light acrylic freestanding tub (27–45 kg) largely removes the structural question — one reason it's the default choice upstairs.

5 · Style and resale

A freestanding tub is the centrepiece move — sculptural, photogenic, and the first thing an inspection notices. Built-ins disappear into the architecture, which is exactly right for compact family bathrooms and wet-room layouts. Neither is universally "more premium"; execution decides.

The verdict

Choose built-in for compact rooms, shower-over-bath layouts, and budget-led renovations. Choose freestanding when the room has space to give and you want the bath to be the room's statement. And if your space sits awkwardly between standard sizes either way — that's what custom dimensions and a made-to-order shape are for. Frequently asked questions

Do freestanding bathtubs take up more space?

They need breathing room — at least 100–150 mm of clearance around the tub, ideally 300 mm on the access side — so the same tub occupies more floor area than an alcove install. In generous rooms this is a feature; in tight rooms it's the deciding constraint.

Are freestanding tubs harder to clean?

The tub itself is no harder, but you gain floor to mop and (if placed near a wall) a gap to reach. Choosing full wall clearance or a wall-snug back-to-wall design avoids the awkward middle ground.

Is a freestanding bathtub more expensive to install?

Usually somewhat — floor-mounted or exposed plumbing costs more than a standard wall-fed alcove connection, and a heavy tub may need a floor-loading check. The tub itself spans the same price range as quality built-ins.

Can I put a freestanding tub in a small bathroom?

Yes — choose a compact deep soaker (1400–1500 mm) and give it honest clearance rather than forcing a large tub against the walls. A small freestanding tub with room around it looks bigger than a big tub jammed in.
